The Riverscapes Inventory Report provides an overview of the extent of riverscapes within the selected area of interest. The report also presents descriptive information about the riverscapes (e.g. ownership and flow type), as well as some information on the physiographic setting and physical characteristics of riverscapes that are important for informing expected form and function of streams within the area of interest. An overview of the existing resources is an important first step in assessing opportunities and planning riverscape management and restoration actions.

Ownership

This section provides an overview of the ownership of the riverscapes within the area of interest. Each riverscape segment (DGO) is assigned an ownership category based on which category makes up the largest proportion of the area within the riverscape (i.e., there may be more than one land ownership type present in a given DGO, but it only gets assigned the category that covers the largest proportion of its area). The total area is then summed up for each distinct onwer type.

Flow Types

This section summarizes the areas and lengths of riverscapes within the area that have different flow types. This provides insight into the proportion of the riverscapes that flow year round (perennial), those that flow seasonally (intermittent), and those that only flow in response to precipitation events (ephemeral). 'Artifical paths' are a National Hydrography Dataset (NHD) classification for flow lines that cross digitized polygons. These are typically larger rivers, and consequently artificial paths typically represent perennial streams.

This section provides an overview of the physiographic setting of the riverscapes within the area of interest.

Hypsometry

Hypsometric curves show the elevation distribution within an area, providing a visual profile of an areas topography. Elevation is an important control on local climate and therefore affects various ecological and hydrological processes. So, this curve implicitly provides information on the climate for the area, and consequently the hydrology and vegetation communities that may be present. This curve represents the elevation within the entirety of the HUC10 watersheds that contain the selected riverscapes (i.e., may extend beyond the area of interest). The list of watersheds used corresponds to the projects listed in Appendix 1.

Stream Order

Stream order is a measure of the relative size of streams. It is determined by the hierarchy of tributaries in a drainage basin. In this report, we use the Strahler method to classify stream order, where the smallest tributaries are assigned an order of 1. When two streams of the same order merge, the resulting stream is assigned an order that is one higher. If streams of different orders merge, the resulting stream retains the higher order.

Appendix 2 - Source Data

This report was generated using data from the Riverscapes Data Exchange. Free, publicly accessible data were processed through a series of models by the Riverscapes Consortium, culminating with the Riverscapes Metric Engine (RME) that synthesizes metrics together for all the preceding models. These RME data are based on polygons that span the valley bottom and are segmented at a distance that varies with drainage area. These polygons, or Discrete Geographic Objects (DGOs) possess over 100 metrics spanning a variety of topics pertaining to riverscape health.

The model runs in question were all part of an initiative to produce a continuous riverscapes dataset for the Conterminous United States in 2025.
